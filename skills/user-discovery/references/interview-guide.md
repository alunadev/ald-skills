# Interview Guide — Complete Reference

## Core Interview Script (45 min)

Adapt this — don't script. The goal is a natural conversation, not a survey.

---

### Opening (2 min)

"Thanks for making time. A few things before we start:
- There are no right or wrong answers — I'm here to learn from your experience
- I may ask follow-up questions that seem obvious — I want to make sure I understand fully
- I'd like to record this (for my notes only, not shared). Is that okay?
- Feel free to tell me if something is confidential"

---

### Section 1: Context (5 min)

Goal: Understand their role and how the relevant workflow fits their day.

- "Walk me through your typical day — what does [relevant job area] look like for you?"
- "How long have you been doing this?"
- "What does [the product/workflow] help you with, if at all?"
- "Who else on your team does this? Is it consistent across the team or does everyone do it differently?"

---

### Section 2: The Last Time (10 min)

Goal: Get to a specific, real story — not abstract opinions.

**The anchor question:**
> "Tell me about the last time you had to [do the thing we're researching]."

Follow-ups:
- "Walk me through it step by step, from the very beginning."
- "What prompted that? What were you trying to accomplish?"
- "What did you do first? Then what?"
- "Who else was involved?"
- "How long did that take?"

---

### Section 3: Pain Identification (15 min)

Goal: Find the friction, understand frequency and emotional intensity.

After they walk through the story:
- "What was the hardest part of that?"
- "Was there a moment where you thought 'I wish this were easier'? Tell me about that."
- "What did you do when [friction point happened]?"
- "How did that make you feel?"
- "How often does this situation come up?" (daily / weekly / monthly)
- "On a scale of 1-5, how frustrating is this for you?"

**If they propose a solution:**
- "Interesting — what would that let you do that you can't do today?"
- "If you had that, what would change?"
- *(Keep asking until you're back at the underlying job, not the feature)*

**Digging deeper into workarounds:**
- "So what do you do today when this happens?"
- "How long have you been doing it that way?"
- "What don't you like about that approach?"

---

### Section 4: Current Alternatives (10 min)

Goal: Understand what they're actually using today and what's missing.

- "Walk me through your current setup for this — what tools, processes?"
- "Can you show me how you do this? [screen share if remote]"
- "What do you like about your current solution?"
- "What's missing or frustrating?"
- "Have you tried anything else? What happened?"
- "What would make you switch from what you're using now?"

---

### Section 5: Wrap-Up (5 min)

Goal: Capture anything missed and get referrals.

- "Is there anything about [topic] that I haven't asked about that you think is important?"
- "If you could change one thing about how you [do this job], what would it be?"
- "Who else on your team has strong opinions about this? Could I talk to them?"
- "Is there anything you're curious about or would want to know from our side?"

---

## Follow-Up Questions by Scenario

### When they go abstract ("I always feel like...")

> "Can you give me a specific example of when that happened recently?"
> "Tell me about the last time you felt that way."

### When they propose a feature

> "What would that let you do that you can't do now?"
> "If you had that, what would change in your workflow?"
> "What problem would that solve for you?"

### When they pause or hesitate

> *(Say nothing — wait 3 seconds. The best insights come after hesitation.)*

### When the answer is superficial

> "Say more about that."
> "What do you mean by [their word]?"
> "Why does that matter to you?"

### When they compare to competitors

> "What does [competitor] do that you wish we did?"
> "When you say [competitor] is better at X, can you walk me through a specific example?"

### When they mention a workaround

> "How long have you been doing it that way?"
> "What made you come up with that workaround?"
> "If you didn't have to do that, what would you do with that time?"

### When they seem satisfied with the status quo

> "If you were designing this from scratch, what would you change?"
> "What do your most frustrated colleagues complain about?"
> "What's the thing you dread doing most in this area?"

## Stakeholder Interview Variant (Internal)

Use when interviewing engineers, designers, sales, support, or CS — not end users.

**Additional questions:**
- "What do you hear from customers / users most often about [topic]?"
- "What's the one thing customers ask for that we haven't built?"
- "What's broken that you wish we'd fix before building anything new?"
- "Where do users get stuck that you regularly have to help with?"
- "If you were PM for a day, what's the first thing you'd change?"

## Recruiting & Screener Template

**Email subject:** "Quick 30-min chat about [topic] — I'd love your perspective"

**Email body:**
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm [Name], [role] at [Company]. We're doing research on [topic area] and I'd love to learn from your experience.
>
> It's a 30-45 min conversation, no prep needed. Just your honest experience.
>
> Available times: [3 options]
>
> [Optional: Small thank-you — gift card, early access, etc.]
>
> Would any of these work for you?

**Screener questions (send before confirming):**
1. How often do you [relevant behavior]? (daily / weekly / monthly / rarely)
2. Do you currently use [product/tool] for [relevant job]?
3. In the last 30 days, have you experienced [specific pain]?

*Target: Participants who answer "daily" or "weekly" to Q1 and "yes" to Q2 or Q3.*

## Note-Taking Tips

**If recording:** Write only key words during the interview. Full notes within 30 min after.

**If not recording:** Bring a note-taker. Never take full notes yourself — you'll miss the conversation.

**What to capture:**
- Exact quotes (use quotation marks)
- Specific pain moments (note the trigger, behavior, consequence)
- Workarounds (these are gold)
- Emotional signals (surprise, frustration, excitement, embarrassment)
- Things they do automatically without noticing

**What NOT to capture:**
- Your interpretation (capture what they said, interpret during synthesis)
- Solutions they proposed (capture as "proposed X" but dig to the underlying job)
